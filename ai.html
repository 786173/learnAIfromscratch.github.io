6.803 Computing Machinery & Intelligence

Turing Computing Machinery and Intelligence

A paper by Turing, there is also a html version.

I decided to print this paper and read it without looking at a screen. This is extremely comfy when it's late at night and you want to sleep, and bright blue light injected directly into your brain by staring at a large screen before sleeping is never a good idea. You can also take notes more easily.

"A number of interrogators could be used, and statistics compiled to show how often the right identification was given." This is essentially the present state of AI. The chapter on discrete-state machines, how to read that table? Input is ON or OFF. At the top, the last states are the columns. If the input is off (\(i_0\)), and the last state was q1, then the current state is q2. See this demonstration. Turing predicts storage capacity will be so vast in the future the imitation game will be difficult to distinguish from intelligence within the first 5 minutes of interrogation. There's an interesting refutation by example to the argument that a program can't write a sonnet. He concludes that abstract learning would probably be the best place to start, which is where we have already started, with Alpha Go/chess programs. He also suggests a language strategy as a starting point, teaching a program to read and understand a language.

If you look this paper up on Wikipedia, they primarily focus on the specifics of the turing test and imitation game but reading this paper I find that is overanalysis and isn't the main points of this paper, which are to debunk popular objections then to the proposition 'machines can think', not provide any kind of concrete intelligence testing heuristics.

Assignments:

Suppose that you are Alan Turing. You have been asked to participate in a panel discussion on the possibility of intelligent computers at a meetng of the Royal Society. You prepare some talking points by completing the following:

  • In retrospect, the most important part of my paper is …, because…
  • I think the strongest of my various arguments is…, because…
  • I think the weakest of my various arguments is…, because…
  • I hope that people pursue research focused on…, because…don't seem particularly important to me.
